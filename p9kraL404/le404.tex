	\noindent{\bf Task 1.}\\
	\begin{minipage}{0.7\textwidth}
		The triangle spanned by the vectors of the weight force $F_G$ and the downhill force $F_H$ is similar to the triangle of the ramp with the length $l$ and the height $h$. This means that the relationship between the amounts of the forces is as follows:
		$${F_H \over F_G}={h \over l}$$
		The downhill force is the opposing force of the pulling force $F_Z$ and we can equate it with it. This follows:
		$$F_Z = F_H = {h \over l}\cdot F_G = {\SI{1,2}{\meter} \over \SI{3,0}{\meter}}\cdot \SI{600}{ \newton} =\SI{240}{\newton}$$
	\end{minipage}

		\noindent {\bf task 4. b)}\\
		From the force plan we read the downhill force $\vec{F_H}$:
		
		$$\vec{F_H}=\SI{300}{\newton} $$
		$$ a = {F_H \over m} = { \SI{300}{\newton} \over \SI{51}{\kilogram} }= \SI{5,9}{\meter\over {\second^ 2}}$$
		
		\noindent {\bf Task 4. c)}\\
		The friction force is calculated from the coefficient of friction $\mu$ and the normal force $\vec{F_N}$.
		$$ \vec{F_R}= \mu \cdot \vec{F_N}$$
		
		\noindent The following applies to the resulting acceleration:
		$$ a_R = \frac{F_H - F_R}{m}= {\SI{300}{\newton} - 0.15 \cdot \SI{400}{\newton} \over \SI{51}{\kilogram } } = \SI{4,7}{\meter\over {\second^2}}$$
