	\begin{enumerate}
		
		\item
		\begin{enumerate}[a)]
			\item An oscillation is damped if the deflection = amplitude decreases over time and with the number of oscillations and finally becomes zero at some point. This is the normal case with mechanical vibrations.
			
			\item The amplitude is the amount of the maximum deflection; for mechanical vibrations it usually has the unit of a length.
		\end{enumerate}
		
		\item
		The simple relationship between frequency $f$, duration $T_N$ and number $N$ of oscillations applies:
		$$f={N \over T_N}= { 20 \over \SI{5}{\second}}= \SI{4}{\hertz}$$
		
		\item
		\begin{enumerate}[a)]
			
			\item The formula for calculating the oscillation period from $m$ and $D$ is:
			$$T = 2 \pi \cdot \sqrt{m \over D} \qquad (*)$$
			With the values used, this results in:
			$$T = 2 \pi \cdot \sqrt{\SI{0.200}{\kilogram} \over \SI{1.9}{\newton\over\meter}}=\SI{2.0}{\second }$$
			Unit check:
			$$ 1 \cdot \sqrt{ \SI{}{\kilogram \over {\kilogram \cdot \meter \cdot \second^{-2}\cdot \meter^{-1}}}}= \sqrt{1 \over \SI{}{\second^{-2}}}= \SI{}{\second} $$
			The frequency is simply the reciprocal of the oscillation period:
			$$f = {1 \over T} = \SI{0.5}{\hertz}$$
			
			
			\begin{minipage}{0.5\textwidth}
				\item
				The pendulum body is marked in the sketch opposite with a point at which it is located at time $t=\SI{3.5}{\second}$. You can see that its deflection is zero. It came from above and moves downwards through the rest position at maximum speed.
			\end{minipage}
			\hfill
			\begin{minipage}{0.4\textwidth}
				\includegraphics[width=5 cm]{micos127.pdf}
			\end{minipage}
		\end{enumerate}
		
		\item
		According to $(*)$: $T \sim \sqrt{m}$ \quad and \quad $T \sim {1 \over \sqrt{D}}$.
		This means that if we want to halve T, we have to divide the mass by four or quadruple the spring stiffness.
		
		\item
		\begin{enumerate}[a)]
			\item The time that the pendulum takes from the lower to the upper reversal point is just half the duration of a full swing back and forth. So is the period of oscillation
			$$T = 2 \cdot \SI{2}{\second} = \SI{4}{\second}$$
			
			\item The following applies: $$T=2\pi\sqrt{m\over D}$$
			$$\Rightarrow \qquad m= D \cdot {T^2 \over 4\pi^2}=\SI{0.49}{\newton\per\meter}\cdot {(\SI{4}{\second })^2\over 4 \pi^2}=\SI{0.20}{\kilogram}$$
			\item $$F=D\cdot s=\SI{0.49}{\newton\per\meter}\cdot \SI{0.12}{\meter} =\SI{59}{\milli\newton }$$
			$$a={F\over m} = \SI{0.19}{\meter\per\second^2}$$
			\item The speed is greatest in the passes through the rest position. This happens the first time after $t_1={T \over 4} = \SI{1}{\second}$, the second time half an oscillation later, at $t_2= {3\cdot T \over 4}= \SI{3}{\second}$. The third time is half a period later, i.e. at $t_3= {5\cdot T \over 4}= \SI{5}{\second}$
			
		
		\end{enumerate}
		
			\noindent{\bf 6.)}\\
		In a harmonic oscillation, the oscillation period and thus the frequency is, as shown in $(*)$, not a function of the amplitude. So the frequency is always here
		$$f = {1 \over T}={ 1 \over \SI{0.4}{\second}} = \SI{2.5}{\hertz}$$
		
		\noindent {\bf 7.)}\\
		We read the amplitude from both y-axes; it is $\hat{y}=\SI{2}{\centi\meter}$.
		The wavelength can be determined from the diagram on the right, it is $\lambda=\SI{0.5}{\meter}$.
		The oscillation period can be determined from the diagram on the left with $T=\SI{0.2}{\second}$. The frequency is therefore $f = {1 \over \SI{0,2}{\second}} = \SI{5}{\hertz}$.
		The propagation speed is the wavelength divided by the oscillation period:
		$$c={\lambda \over T}= {\SI{0.5}{\meter} \over \SI{0.2}{\second}}= \SI{2.5}{\meter\over \second}$$
	\end{enumerate}
