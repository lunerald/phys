	\noindent{\bf Task 1. a)}\\
	We take a metal plate with a circular hole through which a steel ball can just fit. Now we heat the ball with a Bunsen burner and find that it no longer fits through.
	\vspace{0.5 cm}
	
	\noindent{\bf Task 1. b)}\\
	In a solid, the atoms are arranged close together. When they warm up, they now oscillate more strongly around their rest position. Stronger collisions with neighboring atoms increase the distance. As a result, they gain not only kinetic energy but also potential energy. Both combine to create inner energy.
	\vspace{0.5 cm}
	
	\noindent{\bf Task 1. c)}\\
	First, we calculate the linear expansion coefficient $\alpha$ of steel:
	$$\alpha = {\Delta l \over l \cdot T} = {\SI{12e-6}{\meter}\over \SI{1}{\meter}\cdot \SI{1}{\kelvin }}=
	\SI{12e-6}{\kelvin^{-1}}$$
	The change in length of the rail is then:
	$$\Delta l_s = l_s \cdot \alpha \cdot \Delta \vartheta = \SI{30}{\meter} \cdot \SI{12e-6}{\kelvin^{-1}}\cdot (40- (-25))\SI{}{\kelvin} =\SI{0.0234}{\meter} \approx \SI{2.3}{\centi\meter}$$
	\vspace{0.5 cm}
	
	\noindent{\bf Task 2. a)}\\
	Potential energy of the weight $\rightarrow$ kinetic energy of the weight $\rightarrow$ kinetic energy of the agitator $\rightarrow$ internal energy of the water
	\vspace{0.5 cm}
	
	\noindent{\bf Task 2. b)}\\
	The potential energy of the weight is:
	$$E_{pot} = m \cdot g \cdot h = \SI{100}{\kilogram} \cdot \SI{9,81}{\meter\over \second^2}\cdot \SI{50} {\meter} = \SI{49}{\kilo\joule}$$
	This becomes the internal energy of the water:
	$$E_i = 0.60 \cdot \SI{49}{\kilo\joule} = \SI{29}{\kilo\joule}$$
	This is the amount of heat that the water absorbs.
	$$
	\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{2}
	\begin{array}{rcll}
		E_i = Q &=& c_w \cdot m_w \cdot \Delta \vartheta &|: c_w \quad |:m_w\\
		\Delta \vartheta &=& {Q \over c_w \cdot m_w}\\
		\Delta \vartheta &=& {\SI{29430}{\joule} \over \SI{4180}{\joule\cdot\kilogram^{-1}\cdot \kelvin^{-1}}\cdot \SI {7.0}{\kilogram}}\\
		\Delta \vartheta &=& \SI{1,0}{\kelvin}
	\end{array}
	$$
	The water warms up by 1 degree.
	\vspace{0.5 cm}
	
	\noindent{\bf Task 3. a)}\\
	The amount of heat required is:
	$$Q=c_w \cdot m_w \cdot \Delta \vartheta = \SI{4,18}{\kilo\joule\over \kilogram \cdot \kelvin}\cdot \SI{150}{\kilogram} \cdot ( 38-14)\SI{}{\kelvin} =\SI{15e3}{\kilo\joule}$$
	The energy costs for this are:
	$$K={\textnormal{\EUR{0.04}}\over \SI{1000}{\kilo\joule}} \cdot \SI{15048}{\kilo\joule} =\textnormal{\EUR{ 0.60}}$$
	\vspace{0.5 cm}
	
	\noindent{\bf Task 3. b)}\\
	The amount of water in the shower is only $1/6$ that of the full bath. Since all other sizes are the same, the amount of heat is only one sixth:
	$$Q_d={1\over 6} \cdot \SI{15e3}{\kilo\joule} = \SI{2.5e3}{\kilo\joule}$$
	He would therefore save $\SI{12.5e3}{\kilo\joule}$. This corresponds to 50 ct.
	\vspace{0.5 cm}
	
	\noindent{\bf Task 4. a)}\\
	The amount of heat required is:
	$$Q=c_w \cdot m_w \cdot \Delta \vartheta = \SI{4,18}{\kilo\joule\over \kilogram\cdot\kelvin} \cdot \SI{1,2}{\kilogram} \ cdot (80-15)\SI{}{\kelvin} =\SI{326}{\kilo\joule}$$
	\vspace{0.5 cm}
	
	\noindent{\bf Task 4. b)}\\
	Power = energy per time:
	$$P = {Q \over t} \quad \Rightarrow \quad t = {Q\over P} = {\SI{326040}{\watt\second} \over \SI{800}{\watt}}= \SI{407}{\second} \approx \SI{7}{\minute}$$
